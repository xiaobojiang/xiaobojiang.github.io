The Euclidean Distance is a measure of the straight-line distance between two points in a multidimensional space. For two vectors $A = [a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{1000}]$ and $B = [b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_{1000}]$ in a 1000-dimensional space, the Euclidean distance d is calculated as:
$$d = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{1000} (a_i - b_i)^2}$$

If the vectors are normalized, meaning they are scaled to have a magnitude of 1 ( |A| = |B| = 1 ), this can simplify calculations and lead to interesting properties:

Key Insights for Normalized Vectors

  1. Magnitude Constraint:

Normalization ensures:
$$||A||^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{1000} a_i^2 = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad ||B||^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{1000} b_i^2 = 1$$

  1. Distance Formula for Normalized Vectors:

The Euclidean distance between normalized vectors can also be expressed in terms of the cosine similarity:

$$d = \sqrt{2(1 - \text{cos}(\theta))}$$

where $$\text{cos}(\theta)$$ is the cosine similarity between A and B , defined as:

$$\text{cos}(\theta) = \frac{A \cdot B}{|A| |B|} = \sum_{i=1}^{1000} a_i b_i$$

• When the vectors are identical ( A = B ), $\text{cos}(\theta) = 1$ and d = 0 .

• When the vectors are orthogonal, $\text{cos}(\theta) = 0$ and $d = \sqrt{2}$ .

• When the vectors are diametrically opposite, $\text{cos}(\theta) = -1$ and d = 2 .

Practical Implications

• For normalized vectors, the Euclidean distance is bounded between 0 and 2.

• The distance provides a sense of how “far apart” two points are in the normalized 1000-dimensional space.
