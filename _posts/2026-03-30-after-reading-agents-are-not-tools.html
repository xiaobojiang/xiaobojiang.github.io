The core argument of this piece is deceptively simple but fundamentally important: agents and tools operate under entirely different control models, and conflating them leads to flawed system design.

What stood out most is the distinction around control flow and decision boundaries. Tools are predictable, bounded, and reactive — you give them input, they return output. Even long-running operations still follow this linear contract.

Agents, on the other hand, own parts of the decision-making loop. They don’t just execute — they decide what to do next, often across multiple steps and changing contexts.

This leads to a deeper realization:

Treating agents as tools is essentially trying to compress autonomy into a function call.

That abstraction breaks down quickly in real systems.

Key Takeaways

  1. Tools are deterministic interfaces; agents are dynamic processes

Tools behave like APIs — well-scoped and predictable. Agents behave more like actors in a system, continuously interpreting goals and environment.

Check below example of multi-turn nature of the agent interface, with a tool interface used by the agent.

  1. The real difference is who controls the workflow

  • Tools: caller controls the sequence

  • Agents: control is partially delegated

This is not a technical nuance — it’s an architectural boundary.

  1. “Agent as a tool” is sometimes a convenience, not a truth

While frameworks allow wrapping agents as tools, this is more about interoperability hacks than conceptual correctness. It risks hiding complexity rather than managing it.

  1. Multi-agent systems demand new patterns

The article implicitly pushes toward thinking in terms of:

  • delegation

  • coordination

  • autonomy layers

Rather than chaining function calls.

My Reflection

This reframes a lot of current “agent” hype.

Many so-called agents today are still tool-augmented LLM pipelines, not true autonomous systems. The article highlights why these systems feel brittle: they’re architected with the wrong mental model.

It also explains why emerging patterns (like sub-agents vs. agent-tools distinctions) are becoming critical design decisions in real-world systems.

What This Changes Going Forward

If we take this seriously, building agents means:

  • Designing control delegation, not just tool invocation

  • Managing state, memory, and goals, not just inputs/outputs

  • Thinking in terms of systems of agents, not pipelines

In other words, we’re moving from function orchestration → autonomous coordination.

One-Liner Summary

Tools extend capability. Agents redefine control.
