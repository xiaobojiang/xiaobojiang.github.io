In recent Andrew Ng's newsletter, Andrew talks about AI's acceleration and impact on different software stacks.

Here are the main points and my thinking:

Coding agents are accelerating different types of software work to different degrees. When we architect teams, understanding these distinctions helps us to have realistic expectations.

From most accelerated to least: frontend → backend → infrastructure → research.

Frontend development is dramatically sped up due to strong model fluency in modern frameworks and tight feedback loops.

Backend is harder due to edge cases, security, and subtle bugs.

Infrastructure even less so, given complexity, reliability requirements, and debugging difficulty.

Research is least accelerated—coding is only a small part of the work, and insight remains the bottleneck.

— Andrew Ng

I largely agree with this take—especially on frontend and backend.

Acceleration by Coding Agents (Highest → Lowest)

Where this really holds:

  • Frontend is being transformed the fastest. Tight feedback loops + well-learned frameworks = agents thrive.

  • Backend is still accelerated, but correctness, edge cases, and data integrity make it harder to fully trust AI output.

Infrastructure also feels right as “less accelerated”—not because agents can’t generate configs, but because:

  • the blast radius is huge

  • debugging is opaque

  • trust is still limited

Where I’d extend the discussion: Research

At first glance, research seems the least impacted—and in many ways, it still is.

But there’s an important nuance:

👉 AI doesn’t replace insight, but it can reshape the speed and scale of discovery

Take AlphaFold as example:

  • Predicted structures for 200M+ proteins, compressing decades of lab work

  • Tackled a 50-year grand challenge in biology

  • Now actively accelerating drug discovery pipelines

That’s not just “coding faster”—that’s changing the research loop itself.

So maybe the better framing is:

Coding agents don’t accelerate thinking in research…

but AI systems can compress entire scientific workflows

A more useful mental model

It’s not just about stack layers. It’s about:

How quickly can you verify correctness?

  • Frontend → immediate → high acceleration

  • Backend → delayed → moderate

  • Infra → systemic → low

  • Research → ambiguous → lowest

…but occasionally:

  • When AI reshapes the problem (e.g., AlphaFold) → step-function acceleration

What this means for teams

  • Expect outsized gains in UI-heavy work

  • Maintain strong senior ownership in backend and infra

  • In research: expect uneven—but sometimes breakthrough-level—acceleration

The open question:

If AlphaFold is a glimpse of what’s possible…

which layer flips next?
