The Dragon Boat Festival is called Duan Wu Jie in Chinese. The most popular theory of the origin of the festival is that it was derived from the commemoration of a great patriot poet, Qu Yuan. The festival falls on the fifth day of the fifth month of the lunar calendar.

Qu Yuan was a minister and poet who lived during the Warring States period in ancient China. He was exiled by the king he served and drowned himself in the Miluo River. The locals who admired him raced their boats to save him but failed. They then threw rice dumplings into the river to feed his spirit and keep fish from eating his body.

The festival has been celebrated for over 2,000 years and is now a public holiday in China. It is celebrated by dragon boat racing, eating zongzi (sticky rice dumplings), drinking realgar wine, and hanging calamus and moxa leaves.

Zongzi is a traditional custom for Chinese to eat during Dragon Boat Festival. It is a kind of sticky rice dumplings wrapped in bamboo leaves. There are many different types of zongzi with different fillings depending on the region.
